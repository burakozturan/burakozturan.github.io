This study investigates the ongoing debate surrounding the internet's role as either an echo chamber or a platform for diverse viewpoints. While existing research often focuses on the ideological dimensions of information diversity, this study emphasizes the importance of including sociodemographic variables such as gender, race, and geographic location. By analyzing a longitudinal dataset of 1.6 million representative Twitter/X users over four years, the research provides a comprehensive exploration of news-sharing behaviors and content engagement, offering a more nuanced understanding of information consumption patterns.

The study is structured around three key questions. First, it examines the diversity of Twitter users' information diets, exploring whether users are exposed to a variety of perspectives or primarily encounter content that reinforces pre-existing beliefs. Second, it investigates the structure of the Twitter news ecosystem, analyzing clustering in co-exposure to news sources and how these patterns vary across demographic groups. This analysis highlights whether Twitter fosters interaction across diverse groups or creates isolated information enclaves. Lastly, the research examines the gap between potential exposure to diverse content and actual engagement with that content, providing insights into Twitter's role in either facilitating democratic discourse or reinforcing echo chambers.

By addressing these questions, this study expands the current understanding of how sociodemographic factors shape public discourse and information diversity on digital platforms. The findings have significant implications for the health of democratic societies, highlighting critical disparities in engagement and exposure that influence opinion formation in the digital era.
